All major interpretations rely on floating variables, undefined placeholders such as “measurement,” “branching,” “classicality,” “agent,” or “relation.” These terms function as linguistic escape hatches, allowing the interpretation to continue without resolving the paradox. The UPC audit exposes this pattern: the mathematics itself is consistent, but paradoxes arise when these placeholders are treated as explanatory. By making recognition explicit, UPC dissolves the need for floating variables. Collapse is inseparable from recognition, and recognition is irreducible. Thus, UPC does not add another placeholder; it removes them by certifying the equations directly. 
The recurring pattern across all audits and interpretations can be formalized as a general 
theorem. Theorem (UPC Linguistic Paradox Theorem) No quantum paradox describes an empirically 
real situation. All quantum paradoxes arise from treating the mathematical state description as if 
it were a physical description prior to recognition. Proof. 
1. Empirical Premise: Every measurement yields exactly one outcome per trial. 
2. Paradox Premise: Paradoxes assert contradictory states as real simultaneously (e.g., 
“dead and alive”). This is not part of the data; it is a linguistic claim. 
3. UPC Axiom: Recognition certifies exactly one outcome. Collapse ⇔ recognition. 
4. Therefore: Paradoxes are produced by attributing physical reality to non‑recognized 
states. 
5. Conclusion: Paradox ∉ Physics; Paradox ∈ Language. 
Explanatory paragraph: This theorem formalizes the central claim of UPC: paradoxes are linguistic, not physical. Empirical data always consists of single outcomes, while paradoxes arise only when language misattributes physical reality to mathematical superpositions. Recognition certifies collapse, dissolving paradoxes at the root. Thus UPC is not an interpretation but a diagnostic axiom, exposing that the measurement problem is a linguistic artifact.

# Limits and Next Steps  
UPC demonstrates that the quantum measurement paradox is not a physical contradiction but a linguistic misinterpretation of the quantum state. The paradox dissolves once recognition is acknowledged as the certifying condition for definite outcomes. This resolves the interpretive crisis: paradox ∉ Physics; paradox ∈ Language. What remains is the physicists’ task of formal measurement theory: specifying how recognition integrates with Hilbert‑space formalism, probability rules, and operational models of observers. UPC does not attempt to replace this machinery. Instead, it clears the ground by showing that the paradox was never physical to begin with. In this sense, UPC is a diagnostic axiom: it dissolves the linguistic confusion, leaving physics free to pursue formal measurement theory without the burden of paradox.

Across all camps, the pattern is the same: paradoxes persist only when recognition is denied and
